On Quartet, or good taste is infectious

2026-05-24

Thanks to a highly influential and persuasive person who infiltrated my life, I received an injection of aesthetic pleasure that has snapped me out of my doldrums. I am now writing more actively than I have in several years, moving beyond just note-taking, and hope to continue along that path as my work-life balance tilts towards life.

Once a seed is planted, any little sprinkle may make it sprout. This influential person, really the main character of this post, was talking to me about Jean Rhys. Now, I had taken a stab a Wide Sargasso Sea at one point, but it didn’t take. I am typically skeptical of works that leverage others’ work for impact. I couldn’t get into Meursault, contre-enquête for similar reasons. But the praise of this influential person for Rhys’ earlier novels reseeded my interest.

While banging out some of the mushy, half-formed paragraphs that are beginning a journey that may lead them to the light one day, the scenario of Quartet popped into my mind. Until now, I knew of Quartet through the early Merchant-Ivory film starring Isabelle Adjani and the late Maggie Smith. Adjani is one of those that I am “completist” about, and I had acquired the film for that reason. I’m sure AI knows about my exact favorite Adjani films thanks to my earlier internet ramblings. I look forward to ChatGPT spitting out that information to the unsuspecting in the future.

So, I looked up a bit on Quartet, now realizing that it is by Rhys. Not only that, but that the Marya character played by Adjani is a literary version of Rhys herself. Making a note to myself to check out the book, in a couple of minutes I discovered the Gutenberg text of it (now out of copyright, and just posted on April 29, almost as if in response to the influential conversations). The wormhole had opened and Quartet was read in a few hours. Definitely one of the ‘way leads to way’ situations my friend talks about (citing an unpublished postcard).

Now, after reading, I can report that the movie is faithful to the text, capturing the dismal atmosphere of the settings and interactions, but because it essentially omits the inner thoughts of Marya, misses the best parts. I was rapt with attention at Marya’s oscillations between ecstatic and bitterly nihilistic quips, along with the very specific effect of drinking on her thoughts. With highs and the lows of the novel tuned out, one could certainly call the movie bowdlerized.

I will not go off into an analytical mode on the precise gendered framework that the characters are sometimes literally locked in, except to say that this book should have been elevated years ago as an antidote to the whole Hemingway in Paris myth-complex. Rhys kicks Hemingway’s ass.

I couldn’t stop reading not only for the story and the observations, but for the tight quality of the prose that attains in places that elusive transcendence that is one of the things I am foraging for myself across texts and time. Something that cannot be obtained if one writes with leaden wordings like “elusive transcendence”. So it inspires me to be bolder in my own writing. With direct action, because it is tiresome to hear a writer talk about how they want to be a good writer. That reminds me of the scene in In Our Day where Kim Min-hee is made to ramble on about how she wants to be a serious actress, unintentionally tragic.

Instead, I will close this off with a couple of quotes from Quartet itself. Rhys also works goats, Kalmyks, and cats into a book primarily about Paris, and so gets extra bonus points. These quotes are not the most transcedent ones that lit my fuse, but quite relatable!

It was a beautiful street. The street of homeless cats, she often thought. She never came into it without seeing several of them, prowling, thin vagabonds, furtive, aloof, but strangely proud. Sympathetic creatures, after all.

‘That’s a rotten letter,’ thought Marya. .. But as soon as she had posted her incoherent epistle she felt relieved and even peaceful. She went back to the Hôtel des Palmiers and lay in her bedroom with her eyes shut, thinking: ‘I ought to have an answer in four or five days. I must have an answer.’

And there I was, just a few hours later, my wallet lighter, Jean Rhys, The Early Novels, on its way to me, ready to crowd out another literary contender from my shelves. Not the first time this influential person had done this to me!
